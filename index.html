Building a Brain: A Halloween Neuroscience Experience for Kids

Educational Materials  ·  Live Theater  ·  K–6 Science Outreach  ·  120+ Attendees

Goal

Teach K–6 students the four lobes of the brain and the brainstem through an immersive, theatrical experience — moving beyond passive slides to something kids would actually remember.

Challenges

The entire production ran on a $30 budget. Props were hand-built from materials sourced at home. Volunteers had only 30 minutes available before the event to prepare, so rather than rely on verbal briefings, I co-created a slideshow and explainer video that covered the brain regions, the activity structure, and each volunteer's role — letting them hit the ground running with minimal lead time.

Description

Dr. Frankenstein needed a brain — and a classroom full of elementary school students were just the scientists for the job.

As part of the university's Neuroscience Alliance volunteer club, I conceived, wrote, directed, and fully produced a Halloween-themed interactive neuroscience experience. I played Frankenstein's monster; a co-director played Dr. Frankenstein. All materials — the script, props, and slideshow — were designed and built by me.

Children were divided into five groups, each assigned one region of the brain. Over about ten minutes, each group sculpted their region from clay while discussing its function with volunteer guides. Then, beginning with the brainstem, one brave child from each group placed their clay piece into the monster's head — a painted, illuminated cooler — and watched as electricity dramatically coursed through the creature, awakening each new ability region by region.

By the time the frontal lobe was added, a fully functioning being stood before the class. Dr. Frankenstein announced what each part controls and tested the monster in front of the room. The children had just built a working brain from scratch.

Outcome

Over 120 students attended. The response was enthusiastic enough that parents and children sought us out afterward to take photos and share feedback — an unusual reaction for a school science outreach event.

Skills Demonstrated

  • Science communication for non-expert audiences
  • Curriculum design aligned with learning outcomes
  • Creative direction, prop design, and theatrical production
  • Volunteer onboarding and live event execution under resource constraints
  • Neuroscience content (lobes, brainstem, regional function)

A Student-Led Mental Health Intervention: Research, Design, and Action

Mixed-Methods Research  ·  Community Intervention  ·  AP Capstone  ·  Score 4/5

Goal

Respond to a school-wide mental health crisis with a research-backed intervention plan — designed, led, and implemented independently.

Challenges

Students and faculty were visibly struggling, but the school had no structured response. Rather than wait for institutional action, I treated it as a research problem. When the Vice Principal declined to engage with proposals, I requested time directly with the Principal — who approved them immediately.

Description

I designed and ran a mixed-methods study: Likert-scale surveys of the student body and faculty to quantify perceptions of environmental stressors, plus qualitative interviews with staff coded thematically. I visualised all findings in Excel, identified the most pressing issues, and developed a multi-pronged intervention plan.

To implement it, I founded and led the school's mental health awareness club as the organisational backbone.

Interventions Delivered

  • Bathroom mural painting
  • Community garden
  • Mental health awareness posters
  • Cafeteria games table
  • Awareness meetings on substance abuse
  • Hurricane relief drives
  • Transfer student integration programme

Outcome

A follow-up survey was designed to measure post-intervention change in student affect and environmental ratings. COVID-19 school closure prevented final data collection — but the physical and programmatic changes were in place. This project was submitted as my AP Capstone research paper and scored 4/5, earning college credit.

Hijacking the Nervous System

Gamified Curriculum  ·  Neuroanatomy TA  ·  Semester-Long D&D Campaign

Goal

Make rigorous neuroanatomy genuinely engaging — by turning it into a competition to engineer the most effective zombie disease capable of hijacking the human nervous system.

Description

What if learning neuroanatomy meant designing a zombie plague? As a Teaching Assistant for a neuroanatomy course, I helped refine and run a semester-long Dungeons & Dragons-style campaign in which student groups competed to engineer the ultimate zombie disease.

I was recruited specifically by the professor after taking his class the semester prior — he'd built the game's concept but needed help refining the grading procedure and game design mechanics. My contributions focused on rebalancing the game's underlying stats to allow more creative flexibility, improving group facilitation, and managing the anxiety that came with presenting original scientific reasoning to a room of peers every single week.

Each week, one group member presented their team's pathway choices to me, my co-TA, and the professor. The decisions were genuinely complex: how does your disease enter the nervous system? How does it hijack motor control — fast and energy-intensive, or slow and resilient? Perks had to be argued for and awarded, not given freely.

Across roughly 40 students, the biggest challenges weren't scientific — they were human. Some groups became controlling under competitive pressure. A wave of AI-generated pathways early in the semester flagged students who didn't yet understand the material, which became useful diagnostic information for targeted office hours. The students who struggled most with the game were almost always the ones who needed the most support with the content itself.

Outcome

By the final session, one group came in with handmade props and dressed in costume. The competitive format made learning gaps visible in a way passive coursework rarely does. A few students I worked with in office hours came back to say they'd done better on the exam than on previous ones.

Comfort Cuff — Women's Health Wearable Concept

Product Design  ·  Robotics Coursework

The Problem

Women's pain is chronically dismissed. On average, it takes 8–10 years to receive an endometriosis diagnosis — not for lack of suffering, but for lack of data. This project started with that gap, and for me, it was personal. I know what it feels like to finally have answers after years of being dismissed — and how much sooner that could have happened with better data. Comfort Cuff is the device I wished existed.

The Concept

A discreet, skin-toned waistband worn on the lower abdomen. It integrates heart rate variability (HRV), galvanic skin response (GSR), electromyography (EMG), and accelerometer sensors alongside a manual input button. During an initial training period, users press the button during painful episodes; the system learns their unique physiological pain signature and automatically logs events from there. All data syncs to a companion app, generating a timestamped record patients can bring directly to clinical appointments — shifting the dynamic from "I've been in pain" to "here is six weeks of documented physiological data."

Two Design Tracks

A clinical version optimised for everyday wear and medical credibility, and an advocacy version in which the Cuff transmits signals to a companion purse with a visible LCD display — turning a medical device into a cultural conversation about invisible suffering.

Cytoskeletal Damage in COS7 Cells: An AI-Validated Immunofluorescence Study

Lab Research  ·  Cell Biology  ·  Immunofluorescence  ·  AI Collaboration

Goal

Investigate the cytotoxic effects of a commercial energy enhancer on COS7 cells — characterising both the type of cell death and the structural damage to the cytoskeleton using complementary imaging methods.

Challenges

An earlier pilot using hot tub water as a reagent on U251 glioblastoma cells was creative but ultimately unproductive — it informed the switch to COS7 cells, which offered more reliable maintenance and clearer imaging. At 15% concentration, the reagent mechanically washed cells off the stage during application, making the 5% condition the more reliable experimental basis for immunofluorescence work.

Description

The reagent — a commercial energy liquid containing caffeine, guarana, ginseng, vitamin B3, and Red 40 — was tested at 5% and 15% concentrations. Annexin V / Propidium Iodide staining was used to characterise cell death type, revealing predominantly necrotic death at both concentrations (61 necrotic cells at 5%, 23 at 15%). Subsequent immunofluorescence at 5% showed severe microtubule fragmentation with loss of discernible structure, anomalous actin distribution, and apparent nuclear membrane rupture visible as dispersal of DAPI signal beyond normal nuclear boundaries.

AI was used as a concurrent collaborator throughout protocol development and hypothesis generation, with outputs cross-validated against primary literature in real time. This meant locating the exact language the AI cited within source papers and checking whether it was accurately represented — in several cases it was not. One notable error involved the described directionality of a motor protein walking along microtubules, caught because the supervising professor had authored the paper being cited. These discrepancies were flagged and discussed directly, making AI error-catching an active part of the scientific process.

Outcome

The study produced clear immunofluorescence evidence of cytoskeletal disruption at 5% concentration. The AI validation process itself became a methodological contribution — prompt refinement and real-time literature cross-referencing were documented as deliberate experimental tools, not background aids.

Stereological Analysis of Age-Related Brain Volume Changes in Mice

Histology Coursework  ·  Lab Research  ·  Neuroanatomy

Goal

To determine whether brain region volumes differ significantly between young and old mice — and whether those findings translate to what is observed in human aging.

Challenges

Producing reliable data required precision across every stage of a technically demanding pipeline. Inconsistent microtome blade angles across samples, tissue stretching during mounting, and the subjectivity inherent in manually outlining regions of interest in ImageJ all introduced variance. Recognising and honestly accounting for these limitations was central to the analysis.

Description

Executed the full histological and analytical pipeline from raw tissue to final report. Mouse brain tissue was sectioned using a rotary microtome, then processed with Nissl staining — a classical technique that uses cresyl violet to stain RNA-rich cell bodies, making neurons and their distribution visible under the microscope. Sections were imaged using brightfield microscopy and panoramic photography on a Zeiss Axioscope. Volumetric measurements of four brain regions — the corpus callosum, anterior commissure, amygdala, and dentate gyrus — were then performed in ImageJ using stereological methods, comparing young and old mouse populations. Statistical analysis included t-tests, standard error calculations, and Cohen's d effect sizes.

Outcome

No statistically significant volume differences were found between age groups (all P > 0.05), though effect sizes for the amygdala and anterior commissure were large (Cohen's d = −1.299 and −1.028 respectively), suggesting the study was likely underpowered rather than the effect being absent. Results were contextualised against existing human and mouse aging literature, and a full methodological critique identified concrete improvements — including inter-rater measurement averaging and Stereo Investigator software — for future work.

Japanese Language Club

Club Officer  ·  2 Years  ·  Experiential Curriculum Design  ·  Community Building

Goal

Create a space for genuine language acquisition through real-world interaction — not passive observation of Japanese culture through media, but actual conversation, exchange with people overseas, and low-stakes immersive practice with peers at every level.

Context

The existing options on campus didn't quite fit. As an officer, I helped shape the club's programming around authentic exchange: pen pals with students overseas, a language exchange partnership with a Japanese student who had just moved to our school, and an appearance at the Miami Consulate of Japan's cultural festival where we wrote attendees' names in katakana. Weekly attendance ranged from a committed core group to around 30, with events drawing 45 or more.

Activities I Designed

Konbini Simulator — A staffed convenience store simulation using real Japanese snacks and Monopoly money. Members rotated between cashier and customer roles, handling transactions entirely in Japanese: asking prices, describing products, responding to service questions like "do you need a bag?" Differentiated help worksheets were available for members at different proficiency levels, so beginners and advanced speakers could participate in the same activity without either being left behind or held back.

Shark Tank — Groups of 3–4 drew a problem from a hat (or proposed their own), designed a product solution, and pitched it to a panel of officer "sharks" — conducted in Japanese. The format created natural communicative pressure without the anxiety of a formal assessment.

Tournament Day — A longer session of team-based mini-games: hangman, kanji matching, and trivia, all in Japanese, scored across teams. Competitive enough to be engaging, low-stakes enough that it stayed fun.

Dream Vacation Planner — Groups planned a trip to Japan, with each member responsible for one day of the itinerary. Budget constraints were drawn from a hat — some groups had millionaire budgets, others went car camping. Groups then presented their full trip in Japanese, covering accommodations, transportation, and daily activities. The constraint mechanic meant every group had a genuinely different problem to solve, and every member had a section they owned.

Social Events — Karaoke bar, Japanese arcade, and a potluck picnic where members brought a Japanese dish. The goal was to extend the community beyond the classroom and give people reasons to keep practicing.

Relevance to E-Learning

The materials produced for these sessions — game frameworks, roleplay scripts, tiered worksheets — were designed for participation, not passive viewing. Building an activity that works across proficiency levels, keeps a room of students engaged, and produces actual language use is the same core challenge as designing effective e-learning content: structure that gets out of its own way and lets people learn by doing.
